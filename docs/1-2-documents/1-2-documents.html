Literate programming

Literate programming is writing out the program logic in a human language with included (separated by a primitive markup) code snippets and macros.

  geom_smooth(method = "loess")

Citations

Citation, reference, bibliography

  • A citation marks where information originates in your text.

    Example: Inequality underpins waste management (Kalina et al. 2023).

  • A reference is the detailed description of one source: author, title, year, publisher, DOI.

  • A bibliography is the list of all references in your document, usually printed at the end.

Citations in Quarto

Step 1: declare the bibliography in YAML

---
title: "My article"
bibliography: references.bib
---

Step 2: cite with @key

As @knuth1984literate argued, programs
should be written for humans first.

Inequality underpins waste management
[@kalina2023rich].

Output

As Knuth (1984) argued, programs should be written for humans first.

Inequality underpins waste management (Kalina et al. 2023).

. . .

The reference list is generated automatically at the end of the document.

Citation Style Language (CSL)

CSL controls how citations and references are formatted.

  • Author-date (default): (Knuth 1984) / Knuth (1984)
  • Numeric: [1]
  • ~10,000 styles available in the Zotero Style Repository

Add a csl: field in YAML to switch styles:

---
bibliography: references.bib
csl: apa.csl
---

Building a bibliography by hand is painful. Reference managers like Zotero (free, open-source) export references.bib for you.

Cross references

Cross references

  • Help readers to navigate your document with numbered references and hyperlinks to entities like figures and tables.

  • Cross referencing steps:

    • Add a caption to your figure, table, or equation.
    • Give an ID to it, starting with fig-, tbl-, or eq-.
    • Refer to it with @fig-..., @tbl-..., or @eq-....

Figure cross references

The presence of the caption (Blue penguin) and label (#fig-blue-penguin) make this figure referenceable:

Markdown:

See @fig-blue-penguin for a cute blue penguin.
![Blue penguin](images/blue-penguin.png){#fig-blue-penguin}

Output:

See Figure 2 for a cute blue penguin.

Table cross references

The presence of the caption (A few penguins) and label (#tbl-penguins) make this table referenceable:

Markdown:

See @tbl-penguins for data on a few penguins.
```{r}
#| label: tbl-penguins
#| tbl-cap: A few penguins

head(penguins) |> 
  gt()
```

Output:

See Table 1 for data on a few penguins.

head(penguins) |>
  gt()

Equations

Equations

Note

These slides on equations and equation cross references were added after the workshop was hosted.

  • Write maths with LaTeX syntax between dollar signs.

  • Inline equations sit in the text with single $...$:

    • The slope is $m$ in $y = mx + b$.

  • Display equations sit on their own line with double $$...$$:

$$
E = mc^2
$$

Equation cross references

Add a label (#eq-line) after a display equation to make it referenceable:

Markdown:

A straight line is defined by @eq-line.

$$
y = mx + b
$$ {#eq-line}

Output:

A straight line is defined by Equation 1.

\[ y = mx + b \tag{1}\]
